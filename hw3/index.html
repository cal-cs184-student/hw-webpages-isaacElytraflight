Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

In part 1, we deal with the basic ray generation and scene intersection functions. In ray tracing, we use rays to trace the path of light through the scene. It might be intuitive to think of the ray as going from the light to the camera, but in fact, we instead trace from the camera to the light. This is mainly because our focus is on one specific camera sensor location, rather than the light bouncing off areas we can't see.

I start by creating the function that generates the camera rays. It takes in a point on the sensor grid, normalized from 0 to 1, and then converts it to a ray in the camera space. Then I use matrix transformations to convert that into world space, since I know the exact rotation and translation of the camera relative to the world. Finally, we normalize the ray direction to have a length of 1, and return the ray.

I make heavy use of homogeneous coordinates to simplify the math, so I can package translation and rotation into a single 4x4 matrix.

As instructed in Task 2, I implemented grid sampling, so within each pixel, a certain fixed number of rays are generated in slightly different directions (known as jittering) so that we don't only sample the lattice points in the pixel grid, but also the space in between.

Finally, I implemented the intersection algorithms for triangles and spheres.

Put simply, the intersection algorithms for triangles involve solving a linear system of equations, while intersections for spheres involve solving a quadratic system of equations.

To test the intersection of a triangle involves finding the intersection point of the ray with the plane of the triangle, and then seeing if the barycentric coordinates of that point lie within the triangle. If they do, then the ray intersects the triangle. (We must also ensure that the intersection point is inside the bounds and is not clipped by the t min or t max.) Insersecting a ray with a plane is linear because we have one unknown, t, and one equation: that the dot product of the normal to the plane, and a vector between the intersection point and any other point on the plane, is zero (perpendicular). So, we can solve for t and then use that to find the intersection point.

For spheres, it's a similar approach: we define the sphere as a set of points whose distance from the center is R. Then we replace the vector value of the ray in terms of t into the sphere definition, resulting in a quadratic equation in t. We then solve for t and check if the intersection point is inside the bounds and is not clipped by the t min or t max.

It's also important to mention that, for the triangle intersection algorithm, I used a speedup technique called the Moller Trumbore algorithm, which does the same thing for triangle intersection, but with fewer calculations. It accomplishes this by repurposing some of the math for the triangle intersection algorithm to solve for the barycentric coordinates directly. As a result, it's a bit faster than the naive approach.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

It's clear that the rendering process needs some speedup. Rendering the cow takes 20 seconds on my computer. So, I implemented a bounding volume hierarchy (BVH) to speed up the rendering process.

I use a very simple recursive method for breaking up the primitives into smaller bounding boxes. Until each node is a leaf (has fewer than max_leaf_size primitives), I split the primitives into the left and right children based on the largest axis of the existing bounding box. For example, if the largest axis of the bounding box is the z axis, I sort the primitives by their z coordinate, and then split the list into two halves. I then recurse on the left and right halves.

In this manner, I choose the median of the axis to split on, so that the bounding boxes are as balanced as possible.

If I had chosen the mean instead of the median, outliers or concentrations of primitives would skew the split point, causing the bounding boxes to be imbalanced.

This part was quite long, because I struggled to get the Monte Carlo integration to work correctly.

The way it works is that we sample random points on the hemisphere (sticking out from the normal face of the object we've just collided with), and then projecting new rays in those directions.

We then sum up the radiance of the new secondary rays, weighted by the cosine of the angle between the secondary ray and the normal face of the object.

In true Monte Carlo fashion, we must also divide by the pdf of the sampled points, which turns out to be a flat 1/(2 pi) (since the surface area of the hemisphere is 2 pi).

We also have to make sure to multiply by the BSDF value for the diffuse material (which in this case is also just a constant anyway, as implemented in Tasks 1 and 2).

Analysis (1 paragraph)

Now we can compare the two methods. We can see that the importance sampling method is both more efficient and less noisy than uniform hemisphere sampling. This is because the importance sampling method is able to focus more samples on the more important areas of the scene, such as the light sources.

As mentioned in the homework outline, the hemisphere sampling method will still converge to the correct answer, but it takes many, many more samples than the importance sampling method does.

This mirrors a common pattern we see in graphics optimization: by focusing more samples on the important areas of the scene, we can save compute and still get a good result. This is a similar idea behind the concept of mipmapping, as well as the adaptive sampling that will be implemented in Part 5.

The smoothness of all the importance sampling renders is noticeably better, even in such a small resolution as 480p.

Part 4: Global Illumination

Now we finally get to global illumination. Since we already have local illumination, global illumination is very simple: At collision with a surface, recursively pick a new direction, create a new ray in that direction with depth d-1 (since we already bounced once), and sample the radiance along that ray. I reused my one_bounce_radiance function to implement this, since each bounce needs to independently look at the light(s) and observe its radiance directly from the light(s).

For Russian Roulette sampling, it was only one new lines of implementation: every time we bounce, we take a coin flip with chance p of just returning 0 radiance, and then the recursion tree returns back to the top with those values summed together.

I also found a clever way of implementing isAccumBounces: If isAccumBounces, add the recursive steps; if not, just set L_out to the next recursive step.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

Adaptive sampling uses probability techniques to vastly speed up the rendering process. It's like an upgrade of Russian Roulette ray sampling, but for a different part of the process. While Russian Roulette focuses on exponentially decreasing the number of rays that propogate to another bounce, adaptive sampling reduces the number of total ray samples per pixel.

I followed the instructions in the homework outline to track the sample standard deviation of the radiance at each pixel, and then use that to determine how many samples to take at each pixel. If the standard deviation is beneath the threshold, this signifies that the sample mean has converged sufficiently to the true mean (due to central limit theorem). Then we can stop sampling for that pixel.

As the homework mentions, I calculate a statistic "I", which signifies the size of the confidence interval for the true mean. If "I" is greater than the threshold, I sample again. If it's less, I stop sampling.
